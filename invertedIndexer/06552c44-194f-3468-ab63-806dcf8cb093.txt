<h2 id="mp-tfa-h2" style="margin:0.5em; background:#cef2e0; font-family:inherit; font-size:120%; font-weight:bold; border:1px solid #a3bfb1; color:#000; padding:0.2em 0.4em;"><span id="From_today.27s_featured_article"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="From_today's_featured_article">From today's featured article</span></h2>
<div id="mp-tfa" style="padding:0.1em 0.6em;">
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Louise_Bryant.jpg" class="image" title="Louise Bryant in 2013, painted by John Trullinger"><img alt="Louise Bryant in 2013, painted by John Trullinger" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Louise_Bryant.jpg/100px-Louise_Bryant.jpg" width="100" height="145" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Louise_Bryant.jpg/150px-Louise_Bryant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Louise_Bryant.jpg/200px-Louise_Bryant.jpg 2x" data-file-width="690" data-file-height="1000" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Louise_Bryant" title="Louise Bryant">Louise Bryant</a></b> (1885–1936) was an American <a href="/wiki/Feminist" class="mw-redirect" title="Feminist">feminist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_activist" class="mw-redirect" title="Political activist">political activist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">journalist</a>. After growing up in rural <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a> and graduating with a degree in history from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oregon" title="University of Oregon">University of Oregon</a>, she wrote for two newspapers, the <i>Spectator</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Oregonian" title="The Oregonian">The Oregonian</a></i>. After leaving her first husband in 1915, she married <a href="/wiki/John_Reed_(journalist)" title="John Reed (journalist)">John Reed</a> and moved to <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">Greenwich Village</a>, where she formed friendships with leading feminists of the day. Like Reed, she took lovers, including the playwright <a href="/wiki/Eugene_O%27Neill" title="Eugene O'Neill">Eugene O'Neill</a> and painter <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Dasburg" title="Andrew Dasburg">Andrew Dasburg</a>. Her news stories were distributed by <a href="/wiki/Hearst_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Hearst Corporation">Hearst</a> during and after her trips to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Petrograd</a> and Moscow, and appeared in newspapers across the United States and Canada. Generally in sympathy with the <a href="/wiki/Bolsheviks" title="Bolsheviks">Bolsheviks</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution" title="Russian Revolution">Russian Revolution</a>, her articles featured <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Breshkovsky" title="Catherine Breshkovsky">Catherine Breshkovsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Spiridonova" title="Maria Spiridonova">Maria Spiridonova</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Kerensky" title="Alexander Kerensky">Alexander Kerensky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leon_Trotsky" title="Leon Trotsky">Leon Trotsky</a>. A collection of articles from her first trip was published as a book in 1918, <i>Six Red Months in Russia</i>. After Reed's death in 1920, Bryant wrote for Hearst about Turkey, Hungary, Greece, Italy, Russia, and other countries. The Bryant–Reed story is told in the 1981 film <i><a href="/wiki/Reds_(film)" title="Reds (film)">Reds</a></i>. Her neglected grave in <a href="/wiki/Versailles_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Versailles (city)">Versailles</a> was restored in 1998. (<a href="/wiki/Louise_Bryant" title="Louise Bryant"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<ul>
<li>... that British <a href="/wiki/Suffragette" title="Suffragette">suffragettes</a> <i>(pictured)</i> had to stand in the gutter to sell their newspaper <i><b><a href="/wiki/Votes_for_Women_(newspaper)" title="Votes for Women (newspaper)">Votes for Women</a></b></i>, or risk being arrested for obstructing the pavement?</li>
<li>... that <b><a href="/wiki/Krishna_Kolhi" title="Krishna Kolhi">Krishna Kolhi</a></b> is the first Hindu <a href="/wiki/Dalit" title="Dalit">Dalit</a> woman to be elected to the Senate of Pakistan?</li>
<li>... that <i><b><a href="/wiki/Heroin(e)" title="Heroin(e)">Heroin(e)</a></b></i>, an Academy Award-nominated short film, follows three women fighting the <a href="/wiki/Opioid_epidemic" title="Opioid epidemic">opioid epidemic</a> in <a href="/wiki/Huntington,_West_Virginia" title="Huntington, West Virginia">Huntington, West Virginia</a>, where the overdose rate is ten times the US average?</li>
<li>... that <b><a href="/wiki/Catherine_R%C3%BCckwardt" title="Catherine Rückwardt">Catherine Rückwardt</a></b>, who was <a href="/wiki/Generalmusikdirektor" title="Generalmusikdirektor">Generalmusikdirektorin</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Staatstheater_Mainz" title="Staatstheater Mainz">Staatstheater Mainz</a> for a decade and one of only four women in such a position in Germany, conducted a recording of the First Symphony by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Rott" title="Hans Rott">Hans Rott</a>?</li>
<li>... that <b><a href="/wiki/Emma_Jane_Gay" title="Emma Jane Gay">Emma Jane Gay</a></b>, known for her photographs of the <a href="/wiki/Nez_Perce_people" title="Nez Perce people">Nez Perce people</a> in the late 19th century, is identified as the first American lesbian photographer?</li>
<li>... that <b><a href="/wiki/Chandra_Khonnokyoong" title="Chandra Khonnokyoong">an illiterate Thai nun</a></b> became a highly revered spiritual leader in her community, and more than 250,000 people attended her funeral?</li>
<li>... that <b><a href="/wiki/Annalisa_Crannell" title="Annalisa Crannell">Annalisa Crannell</a></b> brings <a href="/wiki/Chopsticks" title="Chopsticks">chopsticks</a> to art galleries as a tool for finding <a href="/wiki/Vanishing_point" title="Vanishing point">vanishing points</a>?</li>
<li>... that <b><a href="/wiki/Mary_Ann_Kerwin" title="Mary Ann Kerwin">Mary Ann Kerwin</a></b>, co-founder of <a href="/wiki/La_Leche_League" title="La Leche League">La Leche League</a>, said that when <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">breastfeeding</a> in the US in the 1950s, "we would practically smother our babies with blankets to avoid showing any breast"?</li>
</ul>

<div id="mp-otd" style="padding:0.1em 0.6em 0.5em;">
<p><b><a href="/wiki/March_8" title="March 8">March 8</a></b>: <b><a href="/wiki/International_Women%27s_Day" title="International Women's Day">International Women's Day</a></b></p>
<div style="float:right;margin-left:0.5em;" id="mp-otd-img">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 100px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Raymonde_de_LaRoche_Head.jpg" class="image" title="Raymonde de Laroche"><img alt="Raymonde de Laroche" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Raymonde_de_LaRoche_Head.jpg/100px-Raymonde_de_LaRoche_Head.jpg" width="100" height="125" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Raymonde_de_LaRoche_Head.jpg/150px-Raymonde_de_LaRoche_Head.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Raymonde_de_LaRoche_Head.jpg/200px-Raymonde_de_LaRoche_Head.jpg 2x" data-file-width="893" data-file-height="1116" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption" style="padding: 0.25em 0; word-wrap: break-word;">Raymonde de Laroche</div>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/1618" title="1618">1618</a> – German astronomer and mathematician <b><a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a></b> discovered the <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler's laws of planetary motion">third law of planetary motion</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/1658" title="1658">1658</a> – After a devastating defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Northern_War" title="Second Northern War">Second Northern War</a>, King <a href="/wiki/Frederick_III_of_Denmark" title="Frederick III of Denmark"><span class="nowrap">Frederick III</span> of Denmark–Norway</a> <b><a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Roskilde" title="Treaty of Roskilde">was forced to give up</a></b> nearly half his Danish territory to Sweden to save the rest.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/1910" title="1910">1910</a> – French aviator <b><a href="/wiki/Raymonde_de_Laroche" title="Raymonde de Laroche">Raymonde de Laroche</a></b> <i>(pictured)</i> became the first woman to receive a <a href="/wiki/Pilot_licensing_and_certification" title="Pilot licensing and certification">pilot's licence</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/1924" title="1924">1924</a> – <b><a href="/wiki/Castle_Gate_Mine_disaster" title="Castle Gate Mine disaster">Three violent explosions</a></b> at a coal mine near <a href="/wiki/Castle_Gate,_Utah" title="Castle Gate, Utah">Castle Gate, Utah</a>, U.S., killed all 171 miners working there.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/1978" title="1978">1978</a> – <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> began transmitting <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Adams" title="Douglas Adams">Douglas Adams</a>' <i><b><a href="/wiki/The_Hitchhiker%27s_Guide_to_the_Galaxy_(radio_series)" title="The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy (radio series)">The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy</a></b></i>, a science fiction radio series that was <a href="/wiki/The_Hitchhiker%27s_Guide_to_the_Galaxy" title="The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy">later adapted</a> into novels, <a href="/wiki/The_Hitchhiker%27s_Guide_to_the_Galaxy_(TV_series)" title="The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy (TV series)">a television series</a>, and other media formats.</li>

<td style="padding:0 6px 0 0">
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Ruby_Loftus_Screwing_a_Breech_Ring" title="Ruby Loftus Screwing a Breech Ring">Ruby Loftus Screwing a Breech Ring</a></b></i> is a 1943 painting by the British painter <a href="/wiki/Laura_Knight" title="Laura Knight">Laura Knight</a> depicting a young woman, Ruby Loftus (1921–2004), working at an industrial <a href="/wiki/Lathe" title="Lathe">lathe</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Home_front_during_World_War_II#UK_and_Commonwealth" title="Home front during World War II">British war effort in World War II</a>. The painting was commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/War_Artists%27_Advisory_Committee" title="War Artists' Advisory Committee">War Artists' Advisory Committee</a>, and is now part of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_War_Museum" title="Imperial War Museum">Imperial War Museum</a>'s art collection. The painting brought instant fame to Loftus, and has been likened to the American figure of "<a href="/wiki/Rosie_the_Riveter" title="Rosie the Riveter">Rosie the Riveter</a>".</p>
